Scarring Progresses

As time goes on, the abnormal healing process creates more and more thick scar tissue in the alveoli walls and the spaces around the alveoli.

This thickening makes it difficult for oxygen to move across the walls of the alveoli and into blood vessels, called capillaries, that provide oxygen-rich blood to the rest of the body.

When this happens, the body’s organs, like the heart and the brain, do not receive the oxygen they need to function properly.

As chronic ILD with progressive fibrosis worsens, sheets of dense scar tissue replace normal lung tissue and shrink the size of the lung.

Some areas of the lung may be replaced by thick-walled cystic airspaces called “honeycombs,” which may be seen on HRCT scans.


Honeycombing means there is complete destruction of healthy lung tissue, and the areas of the lung where honeycombing forms no longer allow oxygen to enter the bloodstream. Honeycombing and scarring of the lungs result in shortness of breath, coughing, and other symptoms of chronic ILD with worsening fibrosis.

When chronic ILD with worsening fibrosis first begins, you may not experience any symptoms, like shortness of breath and coughing. Therefore, it’s important to remember that symptoms are not the only indication of what could be occurring inside your body.


Even without symptoms present, chronic ILD with fibrosis may still be worsening and causing irreparable damage to your lungs.


Additionally, progression doesn’t always show up on HRCT scans, which is why it’s important that other tests are performed in order to monitor for progression.

HELPFUL HINT

Tests used to detect the progression of chronic ILD with worsening fibrosis include:

  • HRCT scans
  • Pulse oximetry (measured at rest and with exertion)
  • Exercise/walking tests
  • Spirometry (forced vital capacity [FVC])
  • Diffusing capacity of the lung for carbon monoxide (DLCO)
  • Lung volume measurement (total lung capacity [TLC])
  • Arterial blood gas testing
  • Questionnaire, like the University of California San Diego Shortness of Breath Questionnaire
